Scott Family Tree

From Macon, Georgia . . .

The "Scott Family Tree" began when Helen Gertrude Johnson was born September 08, 1928, in Macon, Georgia, to Bennie and Maime Johnson.  Gertrude was the younger of Bennie and Maime's two children.

Gertrude's parents, Bennie and Maime, passed away while Gertrude and her sister Mary Alice were young children. As a consequence, Gertrude and Mary Alice were raised by their mother's sisters and brothers.  Gertrude by her Aunt Dorothy and Aunt Lee Lee. And Mary Alice, by her Uncle Bubba Lee.

After graduating grammar school, Gertrude decided to forego high school, to began earning a living cleaning houses and picking cotton in nearby Thomson, Georgia.

When Gertrude became of age, she married Charles Coleman Scott, taking on his surname and becoming Helen Gertrude Scott. Gertrude and Charles Coleman would go on to have six children together: Catherine, Peggy Ann, Charles C. Scott Jr known as "Butchie", Mary Jane, Larry James and Gregory.

In 1958, after deciding they had had enough, Gertrude and Charles Coleman separated. Gertrude and her six children migrating north from Macon, Georgia to Chicago, Illinois. And Charles Coleman staying behind in Macon.

1928 2004

Gertrude's

Journey

To Chicago, Illinois . . .

In Chicago, Illinois Gertrude's family would grow. She would meet William G. Marshall and over the years, the two of them would have three children together: Anthony, Kathleen and Laura Ann.

As time went by, Mr. Marshall would pass away, and Gertrude would meet Herbert Harris, and the two of them would have one child, Carolyn.

Gertrude's family totaled ten children in all and she would go on to become a grandmother and great grandmother many times over.

Gertrude became a member of Christ Deliverance Center Pentecostal Baptist Church.  As a member of CDC, Gertrude instilled strong morals and values into her children and taught her family about "Jesus Christ".

Gertrude, personally, taught Bible Study classes to her family and told all who would listen about "Jesus Christ".

Looking back, Gertrude realized she had unfinished business to attend to. Having foregone high school to start earning a living, she had not earned her high school diploma years ago, while in Macon, Georgia.

To remedy this, Gertrude attended classes, studied hard and earned her high school diploma.

Gertrude passed away June 05, 2004 in Chicago, Illinois age .  She left her spirits of goodwill, kindness, thoughtfulness, and doing unto others, as she would have others, do unto her--as a guiding light to all who knew her. 1

1 Gertrude's sister, Mary Alice, preceeded her in death, passing away in Atlanta, Georgia on November 11, 1998.

As You Write Your Life Story . . .

Think of your life as roll of undeveloped film. Some pictures will come out good, some not so good, and some bad. Portions of film not yet exposed are future life events that have not happened yet.

As much as we would like to, we cannot change "not so good" and "bad" life events, but we can take steps to ensure they don't happen again". 2

As we write our life stories, let us focus on positive happenings, and accomplishesments . . . not unplesant ones.

What Should You Include in Your Life Story?

The great news is you are the author of your "life story". So, what is included—and what is not included—is up to your sole discretion. If there are events and people you’d rather not write about, then you are completely free to leave them out.

To get us started, I put together a list of common topics often included in "life story" outlines:

  1. Background of mother and father (pre-written for everyone . . .)
  2. When/where you were born
  3. Childhood years
  4. Childhood friends
  5. Grammar School
  6. Teenage years
  7. Teenage Friends
  8. High School
  9. Young adult years
  10. Young adult friends
  11. College
  12. Family traditions
  13. Education
  14. Work experience/career
  15. Hobbies/interests
  16. Entertainment
  17. Family members and relationships
  18. Hopes for the future
  19. Local, regional, national, and world events
  20. Marriage/romantic relationships
  21. Children
  22. Grandchildren
  23. Life lessons
  24. Family tree
  25. Special occasions
  26. Retirement
  27. Places traveled
  28. Traditions
  29. Awards/honors
  30. Spirituality
  31. Medical history
  32. Military
  33. And much more!

Why Write Your Life Story?

Writing your "life and times" story ensures, that when your time comes, your "life story" will be told how you want, and not how someone else envisions it. Your memories will live on!
